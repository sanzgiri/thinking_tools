# Free-Floating Rationales

## Brief Description

Free-floating rationales are reasons that exist independently of any conscious thought or intention in an individual organism. They are inherent in the evolutionary logic and can be observed in animal behavior, such as a gazelle stotting to warn other gazelles of danger without understanding why it does so.

## Detailed Description

Dennett introduces free-floating rationales as a way to explain certain behaviors that seem purposeful but may not be driven by an individual's conscious understanding or decision. This concept challenges the traditional view that all intelligent behavior is a result of deliberate, reasoned thought. Instead, it suggests that many behaviors are instinctual responses developed through evolution to improve survival chances.

The gazelle stotting behavior serves as a prime example. The gazelle does not understand that by stotting, it is warning other gazelles about a potential danger. However, the behavior evolved because it helped the herd escape from predators more effectively. This rationale is "free-floating" in the evolutionary logic but has a clear purpose in the context of survival and reproduction.

Understanding free-floating rationales is important because it broadens our perspective on intelligence and adaptive behavior. It challenges the anthropocentric view that only humans possess the ability to reason and act intentionally, and encourages us to consider a wider range of factors when interpreting animal behavior.

## Exercise / How to Apply

To practice identifying free-floating rationales, you can engage in the following exercise: Logic Detective. Observing animals, try to match their behaviors to the underlying evolutionary reasons and contrast them with the animals' thoughts (if any). For instance, a bird building a nest might be doing so because it wants to attract a mate (evolutionary reason) but does not consciously think about the process (animal's thought).

1. Choose an animal behavior you find intriguing and observe it closely.
2. Consider possible evolutionary reasons for this behavior, such as survival, reproduction, or social interaction.
3. Determine if the animal might have a conscious thought or intention related to the behavior. If so, note what that might be.
4. Compare your findings with the actual purpose of the behavior, whether it is known from scientific research. This comparison will help you understand how free-floating rationales operate.
