# Murder in Trafalgar Square

## Brief Description

Daniel Dennett's "Murder in Trafalgar Square" is a philosophical narrative that challenges the notion of beliefs as discrete, sentence-like entities stored within the brain. It demonstrates how the same belief can be arrived at through vastly different neural states and experiences, thus highlighting the abstract nature of beliefs.

## Detailed Description

Dennett's "Murder in Trafalgar Square" scenario presents four individuals—Jacques, Sherlock, Tom, and Boris—each arriving at the belief that a Frenchman committed murder in Trafalgar Square. Each person has a unique pathway to this belief, with Jacques witnessing the event, Sherlock deducing it from evidence, Tom reading about it in a newspaper, and Boris hearing it from a friend. This example underscores the idea that beliefs are not concrete biological objects but rather abstract properties shared by diverse neural states.

The philosophical significance of this tool lies in its challenge to the traditional understanding of beliefs as self-contained entities stored within the brain. By illustrating that the same belief can result from various experiences and cognitive processes, Dennett underscores the complexity and dynamic nature of human cognition. This concept is crucial for understanding how people form and hold beliefs, especially in instances where those beliefs may be incorrect or based on incomplete information.

## Exercise / How to Apply

To practice this thinking tool, engage in an exercise Dennett calls "The Rashomon Effect." Find a short narrative, such as a news story or eyewitness account of an event. Then, imagine yourself as a detective trying to reconstruct the mental states of multiple witnesses to that event. Instead of simply inserting the belief "I saw a crime," break down their experiences into sensory inputs and prior knowledge blocks. This exercise will help you understand that while output beliefs may be shared, the internal processes leading to those beliefs can vary significantly among individuals.

## Suggestion for Creating an App

An app based on Dennett's "Murder in Trafalgar Square" could provide an interactive, engaging way to practice this thinking tool. The app would present users with short animations of events and then challenge them to construct the mental states of multiple witnesses by dragging and dropping sensory inputs and prior knowledge blocks.

The app might feature a variety of scenarios, each representing different types of events (e.g., accidents, crimes, acts of heroism) and presenting users with various perspectives (e.g., eyewitnesses, investigators, participants). Users would earn points based on the accuracy and efficiency of their reconstructions, with additional challenges and unlockable content to encourage continued practice and exploration.

By gamifying this thinking tool, the app would not only make complex philosophical concepts more accessible but also foster critical thinking skills essential for navigating our information-saturated world.
