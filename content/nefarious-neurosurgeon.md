# Nefarious Neurosurgeon

## Brief Description
The Nefarious Neurosurgeon is a thinking tool designed by philosopher Daniel Dennett to challenge the traditional understanding of free will and coercion. It proposes that if someone, such as a surgeon, manipulates you in a way that aligns with your desires, you are "free" because you are doing what you want. This tool emphasizes that coercion (doing against one's will) is the enemy of freedom, not determinism.

## Detailed Description
Dennett's Nefarious Neurosurgeon challenges the popular belief that free will is the ability to make choices independent of any causal determinants. Instead, it proposes a novel perspective where coercion (being forced to do something against one's will) is the real threat to freedom.

In our everyday lives, we often believe that we are free when we can make decisions without external interference. However, Dennett argues that even if an outside force manipulates us in a way that aligns with our desires, we still feel a sense of control and freedom because we are doing what we want. This is the essence of the Nefarious Neurosurgeon.

For example, consider a situation where a surgeon manipulates your brain to make you desire a specific food. If you then choose to eat that food, even though it was influenced by the surgeon's actions, you might still feel a sense of freedom because you are doing what you now want. This scenario challenges our conventional understanding of free will and coercion.

## Exercise / How to Apply
To practice the Nefarious Neurosurgeon, consider the following exercise:

1. Imagine you are controlling a puppet. The puppet has its own desires, independent of yours.
2. If the puppet wants to go Left and you press the Left key, the puppet is happy because it's doing what it wants (and hence feels free). This alignment between your action and the puppet's desire is essential for this exercise.
3. Repeat this exercise with different scenarios to understand how aligning actions with desires can create a sense of freedom, even when external factors are influencing those desires.
4. Reflect on real-life situations where you may have felt controlled or coerced. Analyze whether the situation was truly one of coercion (doing something against your will) or if it was merely an illusion of control.

## Suggestion for Creating an App
An app based on the Nefarious Neurosurgeon could be designed as an interactive game to make the concept more engaging and accessible.

1. The app could feature a virtual puppet with its own desires and preferences, represented by a series of options (e.g., Left or Right, Up or Down).
2. Players control the actions that influence the puppet's environment, which in turn affects the puppet's desires. For example, if the player chooses to make it rain, the puppet might want an umbrella.
3. The app would score players based on their ability to align their actions with the puppet's desires, thus creating a sense of freedom for the puppet (and hence a higher score).
4. To gamify the concept further, the app could introduce obstacles and challenges that require strategic thinking to satisfy the puppet's desires. This would help users understand the nuances of Dennett's philosophy in a fun and interactive way.
