# Occam's Razor

## Brief Description
Occam's Razor is a principle of parsimony that suggests one should not unnecessarily complicate matters by introducing more entities than necessary to explain a phenomenon. It was first formulated by William of Ockham and can be summed up as "pluralitas non est ponenda sine necessitate" or "entities must not be multiplied beyond necessity."

## Detailed Description
In philosophical terms, Occam's Razor is a heuristic used to decide between competing hypotheses. When multiple theories can explain the same set of observations, the one that requires the least number of assumptions is considered preferable because it introduces the fewest new entities and simplifies our understanding of the phenomenon at hand. Dennett treats this principle as a practical strategy for burden of proof rather than a metaphysical truth about the inherent simplicity of the universe.

For example, consider two theories to explain the existence of life on Earth:

1. Panspermia theory (Assumes: Spores traveled from another planet).
2. Abiogenesis theory (Assumes: Life originated from non-living matter on Earth).

The abiogenesis theory requires fewer new entities to be introduced, as it does not involve the assumption of extraterrestrial life or space travel. Therefore, it is more in line with Occam's Razor. However, it is important to note that this doesn't mean panspermia is incorrect; rather, it implies that, if given equal explanatory power, simpler theories should be preferred over more complex ones.

## Exercise / How to Apply
To practice applying Occam's Razor, follow these steps:

1. Identify a "Mystery" or unexplained phenomenon. This could be anything from a missing object, an unexpected event, or a complex concept in science or philosophy.
2. Generate multiple theories that attempt to explain the Mystery. Each theory should introduce a different set of entities or assumptions.
3. Evaluate each theory based on the number of new entities introduced. Choose the theory with the fewest entities as your preferred explanation, assuming equal explanatory power among all theories.
4. Refine and test your chosen theory by gathering more evidence, seeking feedback from others, and considering alternative perspectives.
5. Repeat this process whenever you encounter new Mysteries or complexities in your understanding of the world.
