# Older Brother in Cleveland

## Brief Description

The "Older Brother in Cleveland" thought experiment, a tool for understanding meaning and content, emphasizes that context is crucial for beliefs to function properly. It critiques the idea of implanting beliefs via neurosurgery by demonstrating how the absence of supporting context can cause a belief to malfunction.

## Detailed Description

Dennett presents this thought experiment to illustrate the holistic nature of belief systems. A belief is not an isolated entity but rather a part of an extensive network of related beliefs and world-relations that provide context. This interconnectedness is essential for beliefs to function effectively. For instance, believing "I have a brother in Cleveland" requires knowing who a brother is, what it means to have a family member, understanding the concept of geographical locations like Cleveland, etc.

The "Older Brother in Cleveland" thought experiment challenges the idea that implanting a belief through neurosurgery would automatically result in the belief becoming part of an individual's mental landscape. If someone without a brother were to have their brain wired to think they do, they would be unable to provide details about their brother when asked, or may even hallucinate imaginary siblings. This demonstrates that beliefs require context for them to operate coherently and that they cannot simply be implanted as standalone objects.

This philosophical concept is important because it highlights the complexity of mental processes like belief formation and underscores the need to consider the broader context in which beliefs are held.

## Exercise / How to Apply

To practice this thinking tool, follow these steps:

1. Choose a specific false belief you want to implant into a character (e.g., "I can fly").
2. Identify the supporting beliefs needed for the main belief to function within the character's reality (e.g., "Gravity doesn't apply to me," "I have wings").
3. Pretend to be the neurosurgeon and insert these beliefs into your character.
4. Simulate a reality in which the character attempts to act on their new belief (e.g., jumping off a roof). Observe how the lack of supporting context causes the belief to malfunction, resulting in injury or a crashing simulation.
5. Refine and add more supporting beliefs until the character can successfully act on their belief without harm, illustrating the importance of context for beliefs.

## Suggestion for Creating an App

The "Base App Idea" could be developed into a mobile or web application with the following features:

1. Belief Library: A collection of various beliefs to choose from, categorized by type and difficulty level.
2. Context Builder: Allows users to add supporting beliefs for their chosen belief to create a holistic network of beliefs that provide context.
3. Reality Simulator: A virtual environment in which the user can simulate the character acting on their new belief to observe the consequences of the lack or presence of proper context.
4. Gamification: Implement rewards, points, and leaderboards to encourage continuous learning and mastery of understanding context in belief formation.
5. Discussion Forum: A place for users to share their experiences, learn from others' successes and failures, and engage in philosophical discussions about the importance of context in beliefs.
