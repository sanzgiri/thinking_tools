# Reductio ad Absurdum

## Brief Description
Reductio ad Absurdum is a classic logical maneuver that Dennett repositions as a tool for exposing structural flaws in arguments through parody. It involves taking an opponent's argument structure, preserving the logic but changing the variables, to demonstrate that it leads to a ridiculous or contradictory conclusion. This technique serves as a stress test for logical validity.

## Detailed Description
The Reductio ad Absurdum is a powerful philosophical tool used to scrutinize the underlying structure of arguments. The goal is to identify any hidden flaws that might not be apparent from a straightforward examination. By applying this technique, one can dismantle an argument by demonstrating its illogical consequences when extended or adapted beyond its original scope.

In essence, the Reductio ad Absurdum reveals the hidden assumptions and presuppositions within an argument that make it vulnerable to inconsistencies or absurdities. It is essential because it helps us think critically about the ideas we encounter, ensuring that our reasoning is sound and our conclusions are well-founded.

Consider a common argument: "We should ban cars because they cause accidents." If one were to apply Reductio ad Absurdum, they might change the variables to a parallel context, such as kitchen knives: "We should ban kitchen knives because they cause cuts." This absurdity highlights the flaw in the initial argument, which is that banning a tool does not necessarily prevent its negative consequences.

## Exercise / How to Apply
To practice Reductio ad Absurdum, follow these steps:
1. Identify an argument you wish to scrutinize. It could be someone else's argument or your own.
2. Break down the argument into its constituent parts, such as premises and conclusions.
3. Change the variables in the argument while preserving the logic, creating a parallel context.
4. Evaluate whether the new argument leads to a ridiculous or contradictory conclusion. If it does, examine the underlying structure of the original argument for hidden flaws.

For example, if someone argues "All men are mortal, Socrates is a man, therefore Socrates is mortal," you can apply Reductio ad Absurdum by changing variables: "All dogs are mortal, my cat is a dog, therefore my cat is mortal." This absurdity reveals the flaw in the initial argument, which is that the conclusion only follows from the premises if all men are indeed dogs.
