# Rhetorical Questions

## Brief Description

Rhetorical questions are a common literary device used to sway the reader's opinion without providing evidence or reasoning. They are often phrased in such a way that they seem absurd to disagree with, but treating them as genuine questions allows for critical thinking and encourages the exploration of alternative perspectives.

## Detailed Description

In his book "Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking", philosopher Daniel Dennett discusses the importance of treating every rhetorical question as a genuine one. By doing so, we can challenge our own assumptions and question the arguments presented to us. Rhetorical questions are powerful tools used by politicians, philosophers, and authors alike to sway public opinion or reinforce consensus. However, they often fail to provide enough evidence or reasoning to support their claims.

For example, consider the rhetorical question, "Who could possibly doubt global warming?" This question implies that anyone who doubts global warming is unreasonable or misinformed. By treating it as a genuine question and asking why one might doubt global warming, we can explore alternative perspectives and assess the strength of the evidence for ourselves.

## Exercise / How to Apply

To practice using rhetorical questions as a thinking tool, try the following exercise:

1. Identify a controversial topic or argument in politics, philosophy, or everyday conversation.
2. Write down a few rhetorical questions that are commonly used to support one side of the argument.
3. Treat each question as a genuine one and ask yourself why someone might disagree with the implied answer.
4. Explore alternative perspectives and assess the evidence for each side of the argument.
5. Reflect on your own beliefs and biases, and consider whether they are based on evidence or simply on the consensus surrounding the issue.

## Suggestion for Creating an App

The "Base App Idea" presented in Dennett's book could be expanded into a useful and engaging app for critical thinking practice. Here's how it could work:

1. Users are presented with famous rhetorical questions from political or philosophical texts. These questions should be phrased in such a way that they seem absurd to disagree with, but the user is encouraged to answer them as if they were genuine.
2. After entering their answer, users are presented with a brief explanation of why their answer contradicts the implied consensus and why it's important to consider alternative perspectives.
3. Users can earn points for correct answers and complete challenges to progress through levels or unlock new topics.
4. The app could include a feature that allows users to submit their own rhetorical questions, encouraging collaboration and community engagement.
5. To make the exercise more challenging, the app could present users with counterarguments and ask them to evaluate the evidence for each side of the argument.
6. Finally, the app could include features that help users reflect on their own beliefs and biases, such as journaling prompts or guided reflections.

By gamifying the concept of rhetorical questions, this app could help users develop critical thinking skills and challenge their own assumptions. It could also provide a valuable resource for teachers and students seeking to engage with complex issues in a thoughtful and constructive manner.
