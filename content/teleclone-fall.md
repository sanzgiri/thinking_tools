# Teleclone Fall

## Brief Description

The Teleclone Fall is a philosophical tool introduced by Daniel Dennett that challenges our intuitive understanding of personal identity based on the idea that survival is about preserving information, rather than physical atoms. It proposes that if an exact copy of a person (a "teleclone") is created and the original is destroyed, the original person survives as long as their informational essence is preserved in the teleclone.

## Detailed Description

Dennett's Teleclone Fall addresses the philosophical problem of personal identity, which has been a subject of debate for centuries. Traditionally, personal identity has been associated with the physical continuity of a person's body over time. However, this approach faces challenges when considering scenarios like teleportation or resurrection, where the body is destroyed and recreated.

The Teleclone Fall proposes that our consciousness, or "psychology," can be seen as a complex information system. According to this view, if we can transfer all the relevant information from one system (the original) to another (the teleclone), the original person continues to exist in the teleclone. This concept challenges our intuitive notion of personal identity and encourages us to reconsider what constitutes "you."

## Exercise / How to Apply

To practice the Teleclone Fall, consider the following exercise: Imagine you are a space traveler with the ability to teleport from one planet to another. In the course of your journey, you encounter a malfunction that results in two copies of you being created at the destination. One copy is flawless, while the other has minor imperfections. However, both copies retain all your memories, thoughts, and personality traits.

Ask yourself: Which of these two copies is "you"? This exercise encourages introspection and helps to develop a deeper understanding of Dennett's idea that personal identity may be more about informational continuity than physical continuity.
