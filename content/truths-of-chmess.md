# Truths of Chmess

## Brief Description

The "Truths of Chmess" is a philosophy-based thinking tool designed to help individuals avoid trivial pursuits and focus on what truly matters in life. It is inspired by the game of Chmess, a variant of chess where the king moves two squares at a time, which has an infinite number of possible games but no value or reward.

## Detailed Description

Dennett uses Chmess as a metaphor to highlight the importance of distinguishing between meaningful and meaningless pursuits in philosophy (and life in general). Just like playing an endless game of Chmess without any purpose or reward, one might spend years delving into seemingly profound philosophical questions that lead nowhere.

The "Truths of Chmess" encourages critical thinking to determine the relevance and significance of the questions we ponder. It emphasizes finding value in our intellectual endeavors by focusing on solving problems that have practical applications or contribute to a deeper understanding of the world.

## Exercise / How to Apply

To practice this tool, one should apply critical thinking when approaching philosophical questions. Ask yourself whether your line of inquiry is leading you towards meaningful insights or is simply an endless pursuit with no practical application. If you find yourself stuck in a "Chmess"-like game, consider stepping back and reassessing the relevance of your question or problem.

A practical exercise to practice this tool could be:
1. Choose a philosophical problem or question that has puzzled you for some time.
2. Analyze whether it leads to any significant insights, addresses real-world issues, or contributes to a deeper understanding of life and the universe.
3. If your analysis reveals that the question is more like Chmess (trivial or meaningless), try finding another problem or question that is more worthwhile.
4. Repeat this process until you find a meaningful philosophical pursuit.

## Suggestion for Creating an App

A potential application for gamifying the "Truths of Chmess" could be a mobile app called "The Pointless Journey." The app would present users with various philosophical questions, problems, and debates. Users can rate each question based on its relevance, significance, and practical applications.

* **Features:**
 1. A library of philosophical questions, categorized by topic (e.g., metaphysics, ethics, epistemology)
 2. User ratings for each question to determine its level of significance
 3. Progress tracking and badges for users who consistently choose meaningful questions
 4. Discussion boards where users can share insights, theories, and debates related to the questions
 5. Recommendations based on users' interests and previous choices
* **How it works:**
  1. Users browse the library of philosophical questions and select one to rate based on its significance.
  2. After rating a question, users can view others' ratings and engage in discussions about the topic.
  3. The app tracks users' progress and rewards them with badges for choosing meaningful questions consistently.
  4. Users can explore new topics or refine their interests by browsing recommendations based on their previous choices.
